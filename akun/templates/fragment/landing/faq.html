Frequently Asked Questions

Stunting and wasting are both forms of malnutrition but represent different conditions:

  • Stunting is low height-for-age, indicating chronic malnutrition over time
  • Wasting is low weight-for-height, indicating acute malnutrition or recent weight loss
  • Stunting reflects long-term nutritional deprivation
  • Wasting often results from severe food shortage or disease
  • A child can be stunted but not wasted, or wasted but not stunted

While the first 1000 days are most critical, some catch-up growth is possible with proper interventions:

  • Complete reversal is difficult after age 2-3
  • Improved nutrition can lead to partial catch-up growth
  • Early intervention (before age 5) shows best results
  • Cognitive improvements are possible even with limited physical catch-up
  • Adolescent growth spurts may provide another opportunity for some catch-up

The focus should be on preventing stunting in the first place, as effects are largely irreversible.

Identifying stunting requires proper growth monitoring:

  • Regular measurement of height/length and comparison to WHO growth standards
  • Height-for-age below -2 standard deviations indicates stunting
  • Slowed growth velocity over time
  • Delayed developmental milestones
  • Consultation with pediatrician for proper assessment

Parents should maintain regular health check-ups and growth monitoring records.

A diverse, nutrient-rich diet is essential for preventing stunting:

  • Breastmilk - provides ideal nutrition for infants
  • Animal-source foods - eggs, meat, fish, dairy (rich in protein and micronutrients)
  • Fortified foods - cereals with added iron and vitamins
  • Fruits and vegetables - provide essential vitamins and minerals
  • Legumes and nuts - good plant-based protein sources
  • Healthy fats - important for brain development

Diet diversity is more important than quantity alone.
